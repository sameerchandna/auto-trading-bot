Great point. You're right — daily turning bearish while weekly is still bullish/ranging is ambiguous. It could be an early reversal or just a pullback. My original Fix 1 handles the easy case (weekly ranging + daily directional) but doesn't address this nuance.

The real problem is that get_htf_bias() collapses two timeframes into a single enum — you lose the "daily is starting to disagree" information. A better approach would be to score daily and weekly separately in the confluence scorer rather than relying on a single aggregated bias.

Something like:
  - Daily and weekly agree → full score (1.0) — clear trend
  - Daily directional, weekly ranging → high score (0.8) — likely early trend or weekly catching up
  - Daily opposes weekly → moderate score (0.5) — could be reversal starting or just a pullback; not enough to block the trade but not full conviction either
  - Both ranging → low score (0.3)
  - HTF opposes your direction → zero (0.0)
This way, a bearish daily + bullish weekly doesn't kill a short entirely (gets 0.5 × 0.33 = 0.165 from HTF) — it acknowledges the daily might be leading the reversal, while not giving full confidence since the weekly hasn't confirmed yet.

This would replace both Fix 1 and Fix 2 — we'd stop using get_htf_bias() in the scorer and look at daily/weekly directly.

The metrics explained:
----------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PF (Profit Factor) = gross wins / gross losses. PF of 1.46 means for every £1 lost, you made £1.46 back. Above 1.0 = profitable, above 1.5 = good, above 2.0 = excellent.

Sharpe Ratio = risk-adjusted return. Measures how much return you get per unit of volatility. Above 0 = positive risk-adjusted, above 1.0 = strong. Negative means the volatility outweighs the returns — you're on a bumpy ride for modest gains.

Expectancy = average pips you expect to make per trade. Formula: (WR × Avg Win) - ((1-WR) × Avg Loss). Higher = each trade is worth more on average.

Why that matters in live trading:
----------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Compounding risk — at 0.30, you're taking 1,677 trades with a thinner edge. One bad streak hits harder. A PF of 1.30 is fragile — slippage, spread widening, or slightly different live conditions could push it below 1.0.

PF 1.46 has more safety margin — real trading always performs worse than backtest. A 0.46 buffer above breakeven is much safer than 0.30.
The DD tells the story — 0.30 already hits 34% DD despite more trades smoothing the curve. In live, that could easily become 40%+.
That said — you might be right. If you believe the edge is real and stable, more trades = more money. It depends on your risk tolerance.
